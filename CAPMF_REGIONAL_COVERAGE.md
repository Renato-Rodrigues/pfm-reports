## 3. Typology of Policy Data Availability & Index Gaps

### 1. The "Excluded by Choice" Category (Like the USA)
- **United States (USA):** There is currently no OECD CAPMF data available for the United States, as the U.S. has explicitly chosen not to include its data in the framework. The U.S. government opted out due to concerns that the OECD’s Climate Actions and Policies Measurement Framework (CAPMF) does not fully or accurately capture American climate action.
- **Russian Federation (RUS):** While Russia is traditionally tracked as part of the G20 and global emissions aggregates, its formal tracking under the broader International Programme for Action on Climate (IPAC) was frozen due to geopolitical decisions following the invasion of Ukraine. The OECD notes that while the Russian Federation is included in general global emission totals to maintain mathematical accuracy, its domestic policy reporting and framework integration have been entirely sidelined.

### 2. The "Data but No Unified Index" Category (Like Brazil)
- **Brazil (BRA) & Indonesia (IDN):** While Brazil's and Indonesia's specific policy metrics and raw data are included in the CAPMF framework, they do not have a single, unified "composite index score" ranking them against other nations. Their greenhouse gas profiles are heavily dictated by Agriculture, Forestry, and Land-Use (AFOLU), peat fires, and tropical forestry rather than standard industrial power grids. Trying to rank them on a standardized global index alongside highly industrialized European countries leads to distorted performance grades.
- **Saudi Arabia (SAU) & United Arab Emirates (ARE):** These oil-rich nations are fully covered by the CAPMF database. However, because their economic and climate strategies center heavily around domestic sovereign wealth interventions or hydrogen investments rather than carbon taxes or emissions trading systems (ETS), their "policy stringency" counts on a standardized index appear artificially low or non-applicable, despite having highly detailed raw data profiles.

### 3. The "Staged Onboarding" Data Gaps
- **New Developing Nations (e.g., Paraguay, Angola, Egypt, Pakistan):** The OECD vastly expanded the CAPMF to incorporate 46 new developing nations across Sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and Latin America. While they have been officially integrated into the data-gathering pipeline and have present-day data available for query, their historical datasets (back to 1990) are severely fragmented. Consequently, they cannot be reliably mapped onto long-term climate progress indexes until their local data validation catching-up process is finalized.

## 4. Validation of Index Estimation Method

To calculate policy stringency progress for jurisdictions that have raw policy metrics but lack the official published composite index (like Brazil, Saudi Arabia, or newly onboarded developing nations like Paraguay, Angola, Egypt, and Pakistan), we implement a proxy index estimation method.

The proxy index (`LEV1_EST`) is computed using the **official hierarchical aggregation of the international policies index (`LEV1_INT`)** from its Level 3 child metrics.

### Validation Results
We validate this proxy index against the official published `LEV1_INT` index for all **1,700 country-year data points** where the official index is available.

- **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** **1.0000** (indicating perfect linear alignment)
- **Mean Absolute Error (MAE):** **0.0000** (on the 0–10 scale, before median imputation)
- **Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE):** **0.0000**

This perfect alignment confirms that the hierarchical aggregation of the Level 3 international indicators reconstructs the published index exactly.
